About the Series

Denji is a teenage boy living in squalor, saddled with his dead father's debt to the yakuza. His only companion is Pochita, a small Devil dog with a chainsaw sprouting from its head. Together they scrape by as unlicensed Devil Hunters, killing Devils for pittance payments.

When the yakuza betray Denji and feed him to the Zombie Devil, Pochita sacrifices himself — merging with Denji's body to become his heart. Denji rises from the dead as Chainsaw Man, a hybrid with the power to sprout chainsaws from his face and arms.

Discovered by the enigmatic Makima, Denji is recruited into the Public Safety Devil Hunters — a government agency fighting Devils born from humanity's fears. What follows is a blood-soaked odyssey through betrayal, desire, sacrifice, and the terrible cost of getting what you wish for.

Story Arcs

Bat Devil Arc

Denji's first mission as Chainsaw Man pits him against the Bat Devil, who has kidnapped Power's beloved cat Meowy. A brutal baptism into the world of Devil Hunting.

Eternity Devil Arc

Trapped in an infinite hotel loop by the Eternity Devil, Denji must fight for three straight days in one of the manga's most claustrophobic and relentless sequences.

Katana Man Arc

A devastating ambush by the Katana Man and Akane Sawatari decimates Public Safety. Denji and Aki seek revenge in a story that reveals the true cost of the Devil Hunter profession.

Bomb Girl Arc

Denji falls for Reze, a girl who seems too good to be true — because she is. Their doomed romance and explosive battles form the series' most bittersweet arc.

International Assassins Arc

Assassins from around the world descend on Japan to claim the Chainsaw Man's heart. Features the terrifying Darkness Devil and a journey into Hell itself.

Control Devil Arc

The shattering finale of Part 1. Makima's true identity and motives are revealed, leading to a heartbreaking climax that redefines everything that came before.

Themes & Legacy

Chainsaw Man is, at its core, a story about desire — and what happens when getting what you want costs you everything you are. Tatsuki Fujimoto strips the shonen genre down to its bleeding bones, replacing power fantasies with a protagonist who just wants to eat toast with jam and touch a girl. Denji's simplicity isn't played for laughs; it's the series' most radical statement. In a genre built on ambition, Chainsaw Man asks: what if the hero's dreams are small, human, and heartbreakingly ordinary?

The Commodification of Fear

Devils in Chainsaw Man grow stronger when humans fear the concept they embody. The Gun Devil — an apocalyptic force of nature — is a direct commentary on how media amplifies fear, and how that fear becomes a resource to be weaponized by governments and institutions. Fear is currency. Devils are the inflation.

Desire as Self-Destruction

Every contract in this world demands a price. Aki sacrifices years of his lifespan; Himeno gives her right eye; Denji gives his heart — literally. Fujimoto draws a direct line between wanting something and losing a piece of yourself to get it. The series suggests that the most dangerous Devil isn't fear — it's longing.

The Cost of Power

Makima embodies control disguised as care. She offers Denji everything he wants — food, shelter, affection — in exchange for obedience. It's a parable about abusive power structures that resonates far beyond manga: how easily people surrender autonomy for comfort, and how institutions exploit the desperate.

Postmodern Shonen

Fujimoto is a cinephile and it shows. Chainsaw Man is packed with references to Tarantino, Lynch, and particularly the Texas Chain Saw Massacre. But beyond homage, Fujimoto deconstructs shonen tropes with surgical precision. The training arcs are replaced by trauma. The power-ups come with amputations. The final battle isn't won through friendship — it's won through a deeply unsettling act of consumption. Chainsaw Man doesn't subvert expectations; it chainsaws through them.
